How Australia’s Population is Changing

Growth, Migration & Regional Shifts

Overview Growth Migration People Key Takeaways

Australia's population is not only growing — it is shifting. This story follows where Australians live, how migration is reshaping growth, and why some regions are gaining people while others fall behind.

Where Australians Live

Population is concentrated in a handful of states, but growth is gradually shifting toward Queensland and Western Australia.

The map and bars show NSW at 8.6M and Victoria at 7.0M in 2025 — together still more than half the country, with Queensland and WA gaining share on the margins.

How Australia Has Changed

Australia's population has grown steadily for decades, but recent growth is driven increasingly by overseas migration rather than natural increase.

NSW’s line stays on top through 2025 (see callout). Beside it, the green overseas-migration band widens sharply after 2021 — near 129K in 2024 — while natural increase stays flat near 28–34K.

Who is Shaping Modern Australia?

Recent migration has become increasingly concentrated among a few source countries, while skilled migration remains the dominant pathway into Australia.

India leads 2024–25 arrivals at roughly 56,000 — ahead of the Philippines and Nepal on the lollipop chart — while the stacked bars show the skill stream still supplies most permanent places.

Who is Moving, Ageing, and Where?

Australia's population is ageing, capital cities continue to outgrow surrounding regions, and interstate migration is increasingly favouring Queensland and Western Australia.

Queensland posts the strongest interstate gain (about +25K in 2024–25; see chart 10), while the pyramid bulge shifts older and Sydney and Melbourne still outpace their regions on the dumbbells.

Population
